Tax season reliability — two consecutive peaks at 100% availability

Topic · Intuit · OINP / Customer Communications

Problem statement

OINP delivers tax-season notifications across Email, SMS, Push, Tray and In-App to tens of millions of TurboTax / QBO / Credit Karma users. Tax peak (Jan–April for US; year-end Dec for some flows) is the highest-stakes window of the year — a notification that doesn't go out can mean a customer misses a filing deadline or a refund update. Historically the system had availability blips during peak that the team would chase post-hoc.

I owned the engineering-side reliability program for two consecutive tax seasons — TY24 (Jan–Apr 2025) and TY25 (Jan–Apr 2026). Both peaks closed at 100% availability with zero customer-visible incidents.

Context & constraints

  • Two simultaneous platform stacks during TY25. The RFM Revamp migration was running in parallel — we were live on both old (notificationadmin) and new (comms-authoring-service + authoring-delivery-sync) stacks. Either could have failed under peak; both had to stay green.
  • External vendor dependencies. SendGrid (email), Braze (in-app + push), Twilio (SMS / RCS), Pulsar event bus — any one of them could throttle or fail.
  • No "freeze window" in practice. Product teams continued to ship new notifications throughout peak — we needed reliability work that didn't require stopping the world.
  • Cross-org accountability. When a customer doesn't get a notification, the page first lands with us; we then have to disambiguate whether the upstream event was published, whether OINP processed it, and whether the vendor delivered.

What I drove going into peak

The TY25 program (most recent and best documented) had five workstreams I owned or co-owned:

  • Game days for region resiliency. Multi-region failover drills in pre-prod to verify that if an AWS region went down at peak, traffic would route cleanly. I ran the pre-flight ensuring every OINP asset had the required regional coverage and was the on-call commander during the actual switch, evaluating anomalies live.
  • HPA scaling reviews. Worked through every service in the OINP fleet on its Horizontal Pod Autoscaler config — minimum replicas, scale-out triggers, max ceilings — so we'd scale before saturation, not after. This was the "give us confidence at peak load" workstream.
  • Pulsar backup setup. Stood up the backup path so that if the primary Pulsar cluster degraded, traffic could shift without dropping events.
  • Eliminating the legacy scheduling service. The old scheduler was a known fragile dependency. Migrated all scheduled-notification flows off it before peak so there was one less moving part to break.
  • Migration of ~100% of traffic to the new stack. The RFM Revamp work (separate page) had to be done before peak hit, not during. Pushing the migration to completion was a peak-readiness gate.

What I did during peak

  • Extended on-call coverage — weekend rotations and night shifts beyond the normal pager schedule.
  • Single point of escalation for partner teams (QuickBooks, QuickBooks Self-Employed, TurboTax, Credit Karma) who couldn't tell whether a missing notification was upstream, OINP, or vendor — I diagnosed by trace-id, identified the failing leg, and routed.
  • Bounced-email forensics for high-volume notifications (e.g. the perf-test traffic that was creating SendGrid bounce noise from mailinator.com addresses) — chased these down to the upstream owners so they didn't mask real issues.
  • Tray-side debugging when one realm/customer was fanning out to 559 users per call and getting throttled — escalated to the upstream-product team to confirm valid use case, capped the blast radius.

Key decisions & tradeoffs

  • Game days in pre-prod, not prod. A real region-failover drill in prod during the lead-up to peak would have been the highest-fidelity test but risked the very availability we were trying to protect. Pre-prod with realistic load gave us 90% of the signal at 0% of the customer risk. Tradeoff: we'd find out about prod-only configuration drift only at peak; mitigated by tightening config promotion gates so prod tracked pre-prod.
  • Migration to new stack before peak, not after. The conservative call would have been to freeze the new stack at small-percentage traffic until April was over, then resume migration. Instead we pushed to ~100% traffic on the new stack before peak. Reason: the new stack was the one with the seamless HPA + observability story we'd invested in. If we'd kept the legacy stack carrying peak load, we'd be trusting the platform we'd already lost confidence in. Tradeoff: a new-stack regression at peak would have been catastrophic; mitigated by the comms-comparator parity validation work (see RFM Revamp page).
  • Eliminate the legacy scheduler, don't patch it. Each tax season we'd patch the scheduler for that year's issues. This cycle I argued for ripping it out instead — the maintenance burden of the patches was approaching the cost of the migration, and every patch was a fresh prod risk. Tradeoff: bigger upfront migration cost; pays off in every subsequent peak.
  • Single point of escalation, not a queue. Partner teams escalating to the platform often don't know which OINP subsystem owns their issue. Defaulting to "post here and someone will pick it up" creates routing lag at peak. I took the personal load of being the always-responding diagnostician for the first cut, then routed to the right sub-owner. Tradeoff: doesn't scale beyond one person; long-term needs runbook + tooling so newer engineers can do this.

FAQ (for future recruiter agent)

How did you measure "100% availability"?
OINP Service Performance dashboard in Splunk — gateway logs aggregated to per-service availability (5xx / total). For the peak window we tracked it daily and the citation reflects the rollup. Customer-visible incidents were tracked separately via PagerDuty + IRIS escalations; the "zero customer impact" claim is from that side.
Wasn't migrating to the new stack during the lead-up to peak risky?
Yes. The mitigation was comms-comparator (see RFM Revamp page) — every read path was diffed against the legacy stack until parity was solid enough that we trusted the new stack to carry peak. If parity had not been clean by Q4 2025, the call was to freeze migration and run peak on legacy. We didn't have to.
What broke that you didn't anticipate?
The Solr/Cassandra tray init failures (see Notification Tray page) kept generating retries throughout late 2025. We survived peak on retrials, which is exactly the kind of "luck masking a real issue" that you don't want — and I flagged this to the customer-comms team at the time, proposing migration of the 40GB tray data to a managed DB rather than continuing to lean on retries.
What's the one thing you'd do differently?
Game days earlier in the year. We did them ~6 weeks before peak; doing them quarterly would have surfaced regional drift sooner and given us a cleaner baseline. It's hard to convince a team to game-day in the middle of feature work, but the cost of doing it once a quarter is much lower than the cost of a peak surprise.
